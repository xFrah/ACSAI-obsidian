The carnot cycle consists of four stages involving reversible thermodynamic processes.

![](../../z_images/Pasted%20image%2020250727141038.png)

> [!hint]
> The fact that this is a cycle of reversible thermodynamic processes means that we can go back to the starting state with no change in [entropy](5.%20Entropy.md) since nothing is dissipated.
> **Total entropy change of the universe (system + surroundings) is zero**. 
> **No energy is "dissipated"** in ways that would increase the overall disorder of the universe (e.g., [friction](Friction.md), heat transfer across a finite temperature difference).


## Step 1 - Gas expansion (Isothermal)

We connect a hot reservoir to the gas. This reservoir has the scope of keeping the gas at a target temperature.

We then release the piston, and since the pressure is high, it pushes the piston and expands in volume (pressure slowly decreases as it goes up).

During all of this, the gas keeps getting heat from the reservoir, keeping it at the exact same temperature.

By continuously adding heat to the gas, we are making this process isothermal, meaning that the internal energy stays the same and all heat is converted into volume (work) basically.

## Step 2 - Adiabatic expansion

We take away the hot reservoir and wrap the system in perfect insulation.

We let the gas expand. Because it is doing work (pushing the piston) but receiving no heat, it is forced to consume its own internal energy.

As it uses its internal energy, its temperature drops. This continues until the gas cools down to exactly match the temperature of the cold reservoir, at which point we stop the expansion (piston physically shouldn't be able to move further).

## Step 3 - Isothermal compression

We connect a cold reservoir that is meant to maintain the temperature of the gas as it was in step 2.

Now we manually push the piston down to compress the gas again. By compressing it we would increase internal energy, if it wasn't for the cold reservoir taking away the excess.

If we weren't doing this, the gas would be harder to compress because of the high pressure build up, but if we remove the excess energy, the gas won't push back as much as we do this.

> [!hint] Do we actually manually do work on the gas?
> Yes, in the carnot engine we do. 
> In real engines, we store the kinetic energy into a wheel and the wheel will push the piston down.


## Step 4 - Adiabatic compression

We take away the cold reservoir, but we keep compressing. The heat has nowhere to go.
This means the internal energy increases, and we don't stop until it has the same temperature as the hot reservoir we started with.
